The Player's Guide to

Ten Candles


The world is dark.
But we are alive.


Ten Candles is a simple tabletop role-playing game. It's a tragic horror cooperative storytelling game about people trying to survive and fight against the evil and insidious THEM that will inevitably and most certainly end their lives. Though the survivors will most certainly perish, the story told isn't one of death, but one of hope, desperation, and the choices people make in the face of their preordained fate.

Many may question the value of a nightmarish game where you can't win. However, Ten Candles isn't about winning: It's about telling a story about people and who they really are. As players play Ten Candles, they'll explore what remains of humanity, overcome challenges, struggle to not succumb to madness, and, maybe, uncover the truth behind who or what THEY are and what happened to their now unwelcoming world.

This website will teach you the bare minimum rules and guidelines that you need to be able to participate and enjoy a game of Ten Candles.

How the World Will End

Conflicts

Conflicts are what move this game forward and shape the story that the players tell. Whenever a character faces a challenge that requires skill, strength, or wit to overcome, tries to do something with an unknown outcome, makes a risky decision, or is simply prompted by the Gamemaster running the story, the player will need to roll dice and resolve a conflict.

The rules are very simple: If you rolled even a single "6", you succeed the conflict.

Conflict Result



Gameflow

Though you only need one "6" to succeed, the story is built in such a way that the odds of success grow lower and lower.

Initially, the GROUP shares a communal pool of 10 dice and has 10 lit candles. The story continues to progress if there's a "6", but every "1" rolled is removed from the pool.

If no "6"s are rolled, the conflict fails and the current chapter of the story ends. Every time a chapter ends, a candle is darkened, bad stuff usually happen to escalate the plot, and the communal pool of dice is refilled to the amount of candles still lit. These permanently removed dice goes to the Gamemaster.


During conflicts, you are also fighting for control over how the story progresses. If you roll more "6"s than the Gamemaster, you get to dictate (within reason), how the conflict is resolved. Conversely, if you rolled "6"s but you have lesser or equal to that of the Gamemaster's "6"s, though you still succeed the conflict, the Gamemaster gets to dictate what happens to you.

You may notice that as the story progresses, the odds of succeeding conflicts decrease and your chances of retaining control over the story become slimmer and slimmer as you lose your dice, your light, and your hope to the Gamemaster and THEM.

Though the initial half of the game may feel like a dreadful slow burn, the pace will pick up and situations will escalate.


What You Will Rely On

The struggle for survival is hard, but not impossible. Sometimes, you just have to be willing to try, even if you will fail.

Typically, prior to the actual game, the Gamemaster will prompt you to write certain things for the purposes of creating your own character.

The Gamemaster will ask you to stack these four cards in some specific order. You may only use the topmost card of your stack at any given time and you may only use one card at a time.

This is what they do.


Your Traits

This is what you're made of. What defines you.

During conflicts, there may be opportunities for you to fight for a Success. Depending on the situation, you can burn away a core part of your character to raise the chances of survival.


A Virtue is a trait or characteristic that your character is proud of. Typically this trait is helpful, positive, and well-valued in society.

After a conflict roll, if this card is the topmost card of your deck, you may burn it to reroll any "1"s you've rolled.

After consuming the card, this effectively means that this particular facet of your personality and core being is burnt away and no more. Though you don't always have to do this, it's recommended that you work your trait into the story.

Below are just a few examples of Virtues and their narrative usage.

Virtues

A Vice is a negative trait or characteristic that your character is ashamed of. Perhaps it's an addiction, a major impairment, something that they have to deal with, or something that they would like to overcome.

Similar to Virtues, Vices are functionally the same in the fact that, if this is the topmost card of your deck, you can burn it after a roll to reroll any "1"s that you've rolled.

Vices

All in all, the traits are a rare second chance for someone to reattempt to succeed a conflict roll or to salvage "1"s that are about to be lost.


Your Hope

The Moment is usually defined after the Gamemaster gives you a brief idea of what the setting or premise of the night's game is.

The Moment is a personal scene or event that the character looks forward to living and serves as an opportunity to find Hope. It is a reasonably achievable event in the context of the game's story and serves as a guiding beacon for the player's narrative direction. Of course, much like real life, not everyone finds hope or gets the chance to find hope.

Unlike the Traits, you cannot use it to reroll "1"s. If the Moment is the topmost card of your deck and you've found a way to work it into the story, you can declare your intention to live your Moment and call for a conflict roll. This works much like any other conflict roll and succeeds with a single "6".

No rerolls are possible. This is a wager that your character deserves hope.

Moment

Maybe John gets to find a quiet place to read in the apocalpyse. Upon a successful roll, not only is your Moment achieved, you also gain a personal (not communal) Hope Die that succeeds on both a "5" and "6" and is NOT lost on a "1".

Alternatively, John's personal haven might've been infested with Them. Upon a failed roll, however, your hope is lost, a candle is darkened, as if a conflict just failed, and the story progresses to its next chapter with its own consequences.


After Everything Is Burnt

This will be the last card of your stack. After everything is consumed by the fire for that ephemeral amount of heat, light, and hope, only then can you explore what your character is truly capable of. What will you do on the brink of hopelessness, desperation, and fear? That's what the Brink seeks to answer.

Whether or not you choose to succumb to your Brink is your choice, but, for those who do choose to embrace the darkest corners of their being, this is what it does:

After a conflict roll, if the Brink is the only card left in your deck, you can choose to use (not Burn!) the card in order to reroll every single dice in your communal dice pool, including any and all "6"s.

Brink

In order to use your Brink, everything above it must be burned. Only when you've tried everything and are at your wit's end can you do your desperate dance on the brink of destruction.

Upon a successful roll, you get to keep your Brink to be used for another time.

However, upon an unsuccessful roll, your Brink is burned, any Hope die you earned is lost for good, a candle is snuffed out, and the current chapter ends. You have lost all hope and have been consumed by your Brink. How you choose to interpret this narratively is completely up to you.


After the last candle fades, the story ends and all that's left to do is to reflect.
